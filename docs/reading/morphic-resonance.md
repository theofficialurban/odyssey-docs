## Overview

In his 1989 work, *The Presence of the Past*, Rupert Sheldrake challenges the scientific orthodoxy that treats the universe as an eternal machine governed by immutable laws. He proposes the **hypothesis of formative causation**, suggesting that nature is not governed by fixed rules but rather by **morphic resonance**, a process where the past influences the present through a **collective memory**. According to this theory, natural systems—from molecules to animal societies—inherit the **habits** of their predecessors, making things increasingly stable the more often they occur. By framing the cosmos as a **developing organism** rather than a static mechanism, Sheldrake argues that biological inheritance and physical patterns are shaped by **morphic fields** that evolve over time. Ultimately, the text seeks to reconcile the modern **evolutionary cosmology** with a new understanding of how memory and habit function as the primary organizing forces of the natural world.

## From Fixed Laws to Growing Habits: A Primer on Morphic Resonance

### 1. The Big Shift: Machine vs. Organism

For more than a century, our scientific curriculum has been built upon a dual vision of the world. In the 19th century, the prevailing model was the **"World Machine."** In this view, the universe was a vast, mechanical clockwork governed by eternal, immutable laws. While life evolved on Earth, the fundamental realities—matter, energy, and the rules governing them—were believed to be fixed, purposeless, and constant. This model inevitably led to the grim prediction of "heat death"—a state of thermodynamic equilibrium where the cosmic machinery would eventually run out of steam and stop forever.

However, since the 1960s, a new model has supplanted the machine: the **"Evolutionary Universe."** Most cosmologists now recognize that the universe was born in a primordial explosion and has been growing and developing ever since. In this modern light, the cosmos is not a cold, static apparatus; it is a **developing organism.** This shift is so profound that even modern physics is grappling with the "Strong Anthropic Principle," which suggests the universe must have properties that allow for the eventual development of life.

**Key Insight:** If the entire universe is evolving and growing, it is a logical anachronism to assume its "laws" are fixed forever. If nature itself develops, the "rules" of nature likely evolve as well, transitioning from blind, eternal dictates to living, developing habits.

If the universe is no longer a static machine, then its rules cannot be static either—a realization that leads us directly to the logical tension inherent in our traditional concept of "Eternal Laws."

### 2. The Problem with "Eternal Laws"

Traditionally, science views the "Laws of Nature" as external, invisible, and fixed rules that preceded the universe itself. This perspective is rooted in Platonic and Newtonian thought, which posits that mathematical truths are eternal and exist in a realm outside of time and space.

Crucially, the very term "Law" is a human legal analogy. In the 17th century, the founders of modern science believed these laws were literally framed by a "Lawgiver" (God). While modern science has largely removed the "Lawgiver" from its equations, it has awkwardly retained the "Law" metaphor. This creates a conceptual vacuum: we are left with a system of "enforced" rules without an enforcer. A system without a fixed lawgiver may instead rely on its own history—relying on memory rather than a prehistoric code.

As we move away from the idea of nature as a slave to invisible, external codes, we must look at how nature might "remember" its own past through the framework of formative causation.

### 3. Understanding Morphic Fields and Morphic Resonance

The **Hypothesis of Formative Causation** replaces the "World Machine" with a model of resonance. To understand this, we must define two foundational concepts:

- **Morphic Fields:** These are non-material regions of influence that shape the structure of atoms, molecules, crystals, and organisms. Rather than rigid blueprints, they are **"probability structures"** that organize the most likely form a system will take.
- **Morphic Resonance:** This is the process of "tuning in" to the past. This influence is transmitted across space and time based on **rhythmic patterns of activity** and vibratory similarity. It is not an energetic transfer, but a transfer of information.

#### The Radio Analogy

To clarify the relationship between genetics and these fields, we can use the analogy of a **radio receiver**:

- **The Genes (The Hardware):** Like the wires and transistors inside a radio, genes provide the material means for the organism to function.
- **The Morphic Field (The Signal):** The field is the signal carrying the music. It is critical to remember that **the radio does not create the music; it receives and translates it.**

In this view, DNA does not "program" the shape of a bird any more than the copper in a radio "programs" a symphony. Instead, DNA allows the organism to tune into the "signal" of the collective memory of its species. Having established these definitions, we can observe how these fields manifest in biological development.

### 4. Nature's Memory in Action: Case Studies

Biological inheritance is not merely a matter of chemical genes; it is the presence of the past acting upon the present.

#### Case Study: The Beech Seedling

As a beech seedling grows, it does not simply follow a localized chemical script. It is **"tuning in"** to the growth habits of billions of beech trees that have lived in the past. It "remembers" how to be a beech tree by resonating with the **collective memory** of its species. The seedling is a receiver for the accumulated habits of every beech that has ever flourished.

#### Case Study: The Swallow

Think of a swallow's instinctive behaviors—migration, nesting, and feeding. These are not merely coded into the brain like a computer script. Instead, the swallow draws upon the **collective memory** of its ancestors. The past actions of millions of previous swallows are a **"living presence"** within the bird, guiding its flight across continents through an invisible field of rhythmic influence.

**The "So What?":** In this framework, heredity is more than a chemical blueprint (DNA). It is a "second nature" drawn from a pool of collective experience. We do not just inherit our parents' genes; we inherit the habits of our entire species' history.

This realization transitions us from looking at individual organisms to the broader question of how new habits spread across an entire species.

### 5. How Habits Grow and Spread

Because morphic resonance is cumulative, a habit becomes stronger and more established the more often it is repeated. This leads to a radical shift in how we view **scientific repeatability**: if nature is habitual, experiments may actually become _easier_ to repeat over time as the "habit" of the result strengthens.

1. **Chemical Crystallization:** When a new drug is first synthesized, it often takes a long time to crystallize. However, as it is crystallized more often in labs around the world, it tends to form more readily and quickly everywhere. The molecules are "learning" the habit of crystallization through resonance.
2. **Animal Learning:** In a famous 20th-century observation, Blue Tits learned to steal milk by tearing off bottle caps. This habit spread rapidly across Europe—**even beyond the range of all normal means of physical communication.** The birds were tuning in to a new, burgeoning collective habit.
3. **Human Skill:** This theory suggests that as more people master a new skill—such as **wind-surfing**—it should become progressively easier for others to learn. We are not just learning from our own isolated practice; we are drawing from the growing field of everyone who has mastered the rhythm of the skill before us.

### 6. Conclusion: A Living Presence

The shift from "laws" to "habits" transforms our view of the universe from a dead machine into a living organism. In this framework, the past is not a vanished event or a dead memory; it is a **living presence** that continues to shape the world through resonance. We inhabit a universe of developing memory, where every action contributes to the collective habits of the future.

## **The Evolution of Evolution: _From Sacred History to Cosmic Habit_**

### 1. Introduction: The Dual Vision of Reality

The scientific inheritance of the 19th century bequeathed to us a "dual vision" of reality that continues to fragment the modern mind. We have long navigated a world where life is perceived as a vibrant, creative, and ever-branching tree, yet we imagine this tree growing within the cold, static ironwork of an "Immutable World Machine." As the molecular biologist Jacques Monod famously observed, this mechanistic universe is "deaf to [man’s] music" and indifferent to his hopes. We are left with a fundamental tension: while biology is a narrative of becoming, the physical laws that govern the theater of life are traditionally viewed as a purposeless, eternal background of "physical eternity."

This modern tension is not merely a scientific debate; it is the secularized descendant of an ancient historical conflict between the cyclical time of the classical world and the linear journey of Judaeo-Christian thought.

### 2. The First Seed: The Judaeo-Christian "Myth of History"

The most radical departure in the history of ideas occurred when the Judaeo-Christian tradition shattered the "cycles of recurrence" that characterized Greek and Hindu thought. To the ancients, time was a circle—an endless repetition of golden ages followed by decay and regeneration. The Judaeo-Christian vision replaced the circle with the line, reimagining time as a unique **journey** with a definite beginning and a final destination.

The central archetype of this vision is the **Exodus**, a narrative pivot where a people move through a wilderness toward a Promised Land. This transformed the human experience from one of passive recurrence to one of active pilgrimage. As the author of the Epistle to the Hebrews noted, humanity became "strangers and pilgrims on the earth," seeking a future country.

#### **Key Pillars of the Linear Journey:**

- **The Unique Creation:** A specific starting point in time, moving away from an undifferentiated past.
- **The Fall and the Wilderness:** A recognition of current imperfection (the "wilderness") that necessitates movement and effort.
- **The New Creation:** A final destination (the New Jerusalem) where history finds its ultimate purpose and fulfillment.

This religious faith in a destination was the essential theological seed that, once stripped of its divine gardener, sprouted into the secular faith in human effort we call "Progress."

### 3. The Enlightenment: Science as the Engine of Progress

In the 17th century, the "Invisible College"—the precursor to the Royal Society—performed a grand act of intellectual alchemy. Thinkers like Francis Bacon transformed the religious millenarianism of a "New Heaven" into a scientific utopia of a "New Atlantis." Bacon argued that humanity could "recover that right over nature which belongs to it by divine behest," not through prayer, but through the mastery of the experimental method.

#### **The 3 Pillars of the Progress Narrative:**

1. **Human Reason as the New Logos:** The conviction that the human mind can decode the mathematical rationality of the universe, replacing divine revelation with empirical discovery.
2. **The Scientific Priesthood (Salomon’s House):** In Bacon’s _New Atlantis_, the specialized research institute of "Salomon’s House" became the new mediators of reality. These "Fathers" of science replaced the traditional clergy as the authoritative guides of human society.
3. **The Scientific Utopia:** The belief that systematic knowledge leads to the inevitable improvement of the human condition.

**The "So What?":** By the end of the Enlightenment, the Scientific Priesthood had successfully replaced the Church as the driver of history. Human reason became the engine of a secular millennium, laying the groundwork for the realization that if human societies move forward, perhaps biology does as well.

### 4. The 19th Century: The Biological Tree of Life

The 19th century witnessed the expansion of "Progress" into the biological realm, most notably through Charles Darwin. However, the paradigm shift required "fuel" in the form of time. Charles Lyell’s _Principles of Geology_ provided this, expanding the age of the Earth into a vast "geological antiquity." This expansion of time was the prerequisite for the biological tree to grow from a single seed.

Darwin famously preferred the term **"descent with modification"** over "evolution," primarily to avoid the word's original meaning: a pre-planned "unrolling" of a divine blueprint. He sought a theory that allowed for spontaneous innovation without a pre-ordained script.

"Darwin and Wallace solved the mystery of existence... by showing how the blind workings of natural selection could shape all forms of life with no design or purpose." — **Richard Dawkins**, _The Blind Watchmaker_

- **The Watchmaker Shift:** Biology moved from William Paley’s "Designing Intelligence"—where an organ like the eye proved a Divine Watchmaker—to Dawkins’ "Blind Watchmaker," where natural selection shapes life through the impersonal pressure of chance and survival.
- **The Remaining Wall:** Despite this revolution, a wall remained between evolving life and the static laws of physics. Biology was a "tree" growing inside an eternal "machine."

### 5. The 20th Century: The Big Bang and the Evolutionary Universe

In the 1960s, the "Newtonian wall" finally collapsed as cosmology itself became evolutionary. Physics moved away from the "Einstein Static Universe" toward the **Big Bang** model. The universe was no longer a perpetual motion machine; it was a **developing organism** that "congealed from the structureless uniformity" of a primordial furnace.

**Milestones of Cosmic Evolution:**

1. **The Primordial Singularity:** A featureless ferment of quantum energy.
2. **The Congealing Cosmos:** As the universe cooled, fundamental forces "froze out" and matter acquired identity.
3. **The Era of Galaxies:** The transition from a structureless soup to organized celestial systems.
4. **The Anthropic Principle:** A critical pivot where physics begins to look purposive again—the recognition that the laws of physics appear "fine-tuned" to allow for the existence of carbon-based observers.

This shift suggests that the universe is not a rigid container for life, but is itself a process of "becoming." This raises the final, most profound question: If the universe evolves, can "laws" truly be eternal?

The "Laws of Nature" is a human legal metaphor inherited from an age that believed in a divine Lawgiver. Whether it is a new drug crystallizing more easily across the globe or **blue tits** across Europe learning to tear milk-bottle caps simultaneously, the "Habit" model suggests the world is a developing organism with an inherent memory.

The grand narrative of history has shifted from a religious pilgrimage to a biological tree, and finally to a developing cosmic organism.
